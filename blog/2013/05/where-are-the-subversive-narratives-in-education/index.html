Where Are the Subversive Narratives in Education?

May 5th, 2013

So what are the narratives we hear about “disruptive innovation” — or even just innovation — in education? They include things like “education is broken.” The incumbent players in the sector — teachers, schools, professors, universities, textbook publishers, and pencil manufacturers — are largely “resistant to change.” Someone from the outside — and this is key to this narrative — will provide the disruptive innovation that upsets, dare we say “revolutionizes” the entire sector. Technology — here’s another key piece of the dominant narrative — technology has changed, will change everything. Education is broken; technology will fix it.

(Audrey Watters’ keynote address at the Ed-Tech Innovation Conference).

I’m really glad there are a few voices (like Audrey’s) pushing back against many of these ideas in “education reform” which go almost unquestioned nowadays. Of course, public discourse is (almost by definition) shaped by the quote-unquote “dominant narrative,” but this happens to the detriment of real change. It’s easy to see examples of this unfolding in almost every area of civic life (e.g., “The War on ”, ), where there is rarely any nuanced discussion of the issues. The situation is even worse for so-called “slow-motion crises” – like the fact that almost twice as many preschool-aged children die from gunfire than police officers who are killed in the line of duty – but sensible gun reform measures supported by almost 90% of the country and a majority of lawmakers die in congress because our society simply cannot manage to have a nuanced conversation about the issues.

Education inequity, I’d argue, is a slow-moving crisis that only gets discussed in the context of flashy “get rich quick” quick-fix schemes. In reality, the problem is always more complex. But by focusing so heavily on “reform” efforts like (for example) the Khan Academy, we never get to discuss the deeper structural issues that led to the inequities in the first place. Why aren’t students in north Baltimore receiving the same quality of education their peers receive in the rich suburbs of DC? Here’s a hint: it has very little to do with the quality of the teachers, and will not be remedied by access to technology that can “flip the classroom.” These are the subversive narratives we need to hear more about.

Subversive ideas can be uncomfortable, and nuance can be hard. But the alternative is a system (indeed, our current system) which is so horribly broken in many respects that I can’t imagine how a status-quo this shitty could arise except by (slow-moving) apathy or malicious design.

Here is one easy example to get started:

In Baltimore, one dominant narrative is “teacher accountability,” where “accountability” is achieved by measuring student performance on standardized tests.

Exhibit A: this year, the curriculum in some Baltimore schools is aligned to the new Common Core standards, but the state-mandated assessments are still aligned to the old standards which test an entirely different set of benchmarks. Teachers following the district-mandated Common Core curriculum (with its increased emphasis on higher-order thinking) are held accountable for their students’ performance on skills they aren’t responsible for teaching.

The real result? Hyper-focus on last-minute drilling for the test. The students lose, the teachers lose, and the rest of the community certainly doesn’t benefit. Accountability is not a bad thing; but when it is pursued entirely in black and white, nobody wins except the politicians who claim they are “addressing the problem.”

Without nuance, nothing will change. Engaging subversive narratives can draw nuance into the conversation.
