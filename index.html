Motivating the Lagrangian

Mar 30th, 2013

One of the coolest things about physics is that physical laws can be formulated in many different ways! It’s mindblowing to me that the universe has some underlying mathematical structure which makes this the case…

For example, take Newtonian mechanics. We’re all familiar with Newton’s second law from high school:

Solving for gives you paths which minimize some theoretical construct called the action. We can derive the Euler-Lagrange equation using the calculus of variations, basically by demanding that any deviation from our object’s trajectory will increase .1

Or, to strip out the physics jargon, it simply means that *objects in classical mechanics move in such a way that they minimize the difference between their kinetic and potential energies.**

Why do we care about this alternative formulation of classical mechanics? After all, the Euler-Lagrange equation looks a lot more complicated than Newton’s …

Besides the obvious answer, “Because it’s cool!”, it’s worth pointing out that the two formulations essentially answer different questions. Newton’s second law (which, by the way, is also a differential equation in desguise!) is answering the question, “I see this object at point A moving with velocity ; what is its trajectory?” In other words, solving Newton’s equation requires a position and a velocity as initial condition.

In Lagrangian mechanics, we are asking a (slightly) different question: “What path does an object take between points A & B?” That is to say, our initial conditions specify two points along a trajectory, and we want to fill in the rest. Many problems which are completely intractable to solve with become easy in this formalism; again, that’s what makes physics so damn cool!

Why is the Lagrangian Kinetic minus Potential Energy?

I’ve always been slightly bothered by the ad-hoc form of the Lagrangian. The Principle of Least Action at least makes heuristic sense to me, but why should nature choose to minimize the integral of kinetic minus potential energy along a given path? Why not minimize some other quantity?

The Quals: Studying Strategy?

Jan 5th, 2013

The physics qualifier exam is a week from next Thursday. There is definitely a lot of material one needs to know. Here is a breif outline of how I plan to attack the studying.

Use the “Feynman Technique”

The basic idea behind the Feynman Technique is to understand a complex idea by explaining it in simple language. Scott Young sketches four steps:

  1. Pick your topic
  2. Explain it as if you were talking to a novice (kinda like Cal Newport’s textbook method)
  3. If you get stuck, go back to the book
  4. Simplify, synthesize, and create analogies

Section summaries

I haven’t done (really any) E&M for almost two years now. I’m planning to read through Griffith’s E&M textbook, and apply the Feynman technique on sections I’m not fully comfortable with.

Problems, problems, problems…

There are qualifier problems online from tests dating back to 2005, and in printed form dating back into the late eighties (?!). I’m going to do as many of these as possible, starting with the most recent exams.

Top-heavy studying

Again, hat tip to Scott Young. The idea here is to front-load your days with work, doing more in the morning and less in the afternoon/evening. This supposedly helps focus, and prevents burnout. He also suggests structuring your weeks like this too, although with such a short amount of time left, not sure I can afford to take any days off…

At any rate, here’s to a good show! I’ll try a post-game analysis to see how well everything worked for me. I’m also planning a post about an idea I’ve been toying with on how to integrate both tablet and computer for enhanced note taking, à la personal study wikis/the textbook method.
