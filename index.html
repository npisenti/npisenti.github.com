The Quals: Studying Strategy?

Jan 5th, 2013

The physics qualifier exam is a week from next Thursday. There is definitely a lot of material one needs to know. Here is a breif outline of how I plan to attack the studying.

Use the “Feynman Technique”

The basic idea behind the Feynman Technique is to understand a complex idea by explaining it in simple language. Scott Young sketches four steps:

  1. Pick your topic
  2. Explain it as if you were talking to a novice (kinda like Cal Newport’s textbook method)
  3. If you get stuck, go back to the book
  4. Simplify, synthesize, and create analogies

Section summaries

I haven’t done (really any) E&M for almost two years now. I’m planning to read through Griffith’s E&M textbook, and apply the Feynman technique on sections I’m not fully comfortable with.

Problems, problems, problems…

There are qualifier problems online from tests dating back to 2005, and in printed form dating back into the late eighties (?!). I’m going to do as many of these as possible, starting with the most recent exams.

Top-heavy studying

Again, hat tip to Scott Young. The idea here is to front-load your days with work, doing more in the morning and less in the afternoon/evening. This supposedly helps focus, and prevents burnout. He also suggests structuring your weeks like this too, although with such a short amount of time left, not sure I can afford to take any days off…

At any rate, here’s to a good show! I’ll try a post-game analysis to see how well everything worked for me. I’m also planning a post about an idea I’ve been toying with on how to integrate both tablet and computer for enhanced note taking, Ã  la personal study wikis/the textbook method.

EZProxify - UMD Libraries Proxy for Reading Articles at Home

Jan 3rd, 2013

Here’s a cool little bookmarklet you can use to access journal articles while outside of the UMD IP address block:

EZProxify

Just drag that link to your address bar, and you’re set to go! If you find an article you want to read from home, just click the bookmarklet and you’ll be routed through the UMD libraries proxy server.
